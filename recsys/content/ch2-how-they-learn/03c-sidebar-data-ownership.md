Every interaction you have with a platform generates data. Your clicks, views, purchases, dwell times, and scrolls are recorded, processed, and monetized. But who owns this data?

## The Legal Ambiguity

**Data "ownership" doesn't map cleanly to property law.** You can own a house or a car — but owning data is different because data can be copied infinitely, derived into new forms, and aggregated into patterns that no individual "owns."

**GDPR's approach:** Rather than establishing ownership, GDPR establishes **rights** over personal data:
- **Right of access:** You can request a copy of all data a platform holds about you
- **Right to portability:** You can request your data in a machine-readable format to transfer to another service
- **Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten"):** You can request deletion of your data
- **Right to restriction:** You can limit how your data is used

**The gap:** These rights apply to data that identifies you. But platforms derive aggregated, anonymized insights ("users who click X tend to click Y") that are no longer personal data. You have no rights over these derived insights — even though they were generated from your behavior.

## The Value Chain

Your behavioral data flows through a value chain:

1. **You generate it** — every click, view, and scroll
2. **The platform collects it** — stored in interaction databases
3. **The platform processes it** — trains models, builds user profiles
4. **The platform monetizes it** — personalized ads, recommendation quality, licensing to third parties
5. **Data brokers aggregate it** — combining data from multiple platforms to build comprehensive profiles

At each stage, value is extracted — but the person who generated the data (you) typically receives only the indirect benefit of better recommendations.

## Alternative Models

**Data cooperatives:** Users collectively negotiate data usage terms with platforms. The idea is to create collective bargaining power analogous to labor unions.

**Data dividends:** Proposals (including from California Governor Gavin Newsom) to pay users for the data they generate, treating it as a form of labor.

**Data portability:** The ability to take your recommendation profile from one platform to another. This exists in theory (GDPR Article 20) but is rarely implemented in practice because platforms have no incentive to help users leave.

**Decentralized identity:** Web3 and self-sovereign identity approaches where users control their data directly, granting platforms temporary access rather than permanent possession.

## The Practical Reality

Today, the practical reality is:
- **You have rights over your personal data** (in GDPR/CCPA jurisdictions)
- **You do not own derived insights** or aggregated patterns
- **Data portability is theoretically available** but practically difficult
- **Platforms have strong incentives to retain your data** and weak incentives to help you exercise your rights

**Consider this:** The data ownership question is ultimately about power. Whoever controls the data controls the recommendations — and whoever controls the recommendations shapes what billions of people see, read, and buy. Whether this power should rest with platforms, users, or regulators is one of the defining policy questions of our time.
